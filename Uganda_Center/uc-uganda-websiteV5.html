Vision & Mission

A transdisciplinary hub accelerating disease-control solutions across East Africa by integrating genomics, epidemiology, and implementation science.

Our Mission

To develop innovative approaches to complex health challenges through:

  • Understanding gene-environment interactions in transitioning populations.
  • Translating viral oncology findings into effective prevention strategies.
  • Creating precision public health tools for targeted interventions.

This unique convergence addresses critical challenges at the intersection of environmental change, infectious disease, and human adaptation.

Center Design: A Framework for Impact

Structured to maximize scientific innovation and practical impact through operational effectiveness.

Organizational Design

Established as a Ugandan-registered research entity with formal affiliations to UC and Makerere University, modeled after successful international centers.

Governance Structure

Balanced Executive Board (UC, Uganda, stakeholders), Scientific Advisory Committee, and Community Advisory Boards.

Leadership Model

Co-Directors (one UC-based, one Uganda-based), Program Directors for research pillars, and an Operations Director.

Physical & Digital Infrastructure

Kampala HQ (potentially co-located with IDI/IDRC), field research nodes, shared lab capacity growing to dedicated facilities, secure digital data platform.

Implementation Timeline

Phase 1: Establishment (Year 1)

Formalize partnerships, register entity, recruit core staff, launch pilot projects, submit initial grants.

Phase 2: Growth (Years 2-3)

Expand staff/infrastructure, launch training programs, develop data systems, strengthen community engagement, build policy pathways.

Phase 3: Expansion (Years 4-6)

Develop specialized facilities, extend geographic reach, add research domains, build regional collaborations, create funding mechanisms.

Phase 4: Sustainability (Years 7-10)

Transition to majority Ugandan leadership, evaluate strategy, develop long-term partnerships, ensure innovation/adaptation, assess scalability.

Research Program: Integrated Inquiry

Organized around three integrated pillars, designed for cross-cutting collaboration.

Dr. Kathleen Grogan

Pillar Lead

Genome-to-Population Health

Examines biological mechanisms linking environmental transitions (climate shifts, urbanization) to human health at the molecular level, focusing on adaptation and vulnerability.

Key Research Areas:

  • Examining how environmental transitions affect human epigenomes
  • Tracking adaptive genetic responses to climate change
  • Developing biomarkers for environmental exposures
  • Studying intergenerational effects of lifestyle transitions (e.g., in Batwa communities)
Explore Projects

Dr. Katherine Sabourin

Pillar Lead

Viral Oncology & Immunity

Investigates how viral infections (like KSHV) interact with environmental factors to affect disease outcomes, transmission patterns, and immune responses in diverse ecological settings.

Key Research Areas:

  • Investigating co-infection dynamics in changing environments
  • Elucidating viral-environmental interactions affecting transmission
  • Developing improved diagnostics for oncogenic viruses
  • Designing community-based prevention programs for viral-associated cancers
Explore Projects

Dr. Diego Cuadros

Pillar Lead

Precision Public Health Analytics

Develops and applies spatial models, AI, and predictive tools using GIS and epidemiological analysis to identify disease hotspots, predict outbreaks, and optimize resource allocation.

Key Research Areas:

  • Creating spatial models of disease distribution and transmission
  • Developing AI-enabled prediction tools for disease hotspots
  • Designing geographically targeted intervention strategies
  • Building digital platforms for surveillance and response

Cross-Cutting Themes

Unifying the Research Program

Integrated Cross-Cutting Themes

These themes unify the research program, enabling discoveries impossible within a single discipline and ensuring local relevance and practical application.

Key Themes:

  • One Health approaches linking human, animal, and environmental health
  • Climate change adaptation strategies
  • Community-based participatory research methods
  • Implementation science for translating findings to practice

Integration allows, for example, mapping epigenetic markers geographically or correlating viral dynamics with environmental exposures.

Investment & Expected Impact

Leveraging initial investment to catalyze external funding and generate transformative outcomes.

Resource Requirements & Funding

Strategic investment is required to establish core operations and infrastructure, creating a sustainable platform.

Startup Phase (Years 1-3)

  • Initial Request: $1.5 million UC institutional investment over three years.
  • Target Matching: Seek significant external grants (NIH, Foundations, etc.).
  • Partner Contributions: In-kind support from Ugandan partners.

This seed funding establishes operations, supports faculty time, initiates pilot projects, and builds infrastructure.

Growth & Sustainability (Years 4+)

  • Secure major center grants (e.g., NIH P30/U54), training grants (T32, D43).
  • Develop multi-institutional consortia and implementation contracts (USAID, CDC).
  • Pursue endowment development, fee-for-service activities, and technology transfer.
